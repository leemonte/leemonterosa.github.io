Treating Multiple Sclerosis

There's no cure for multiple sclerosis (MS), but medicines and lifestyle changes can help you manage the disease. Work closely with your doctor to find the treatment that's best for you and causes the fewest side effects.

INFUSION THERAPY

Infusion therapy relieves symptoms for longer periods of time by distributing medicine directly into your bloodstream.
INJECTABLES

Self-injectable medications make up the largest category of DMTs. They’re used for the long-term treatment of relapsing-remitting MS (RRMS).
ORAL TREATMENTS

If you’re not comfortable with needles, there are oral options for treating MS. Taken daily or twice daily, oral medications are the easiest to self-administer but require that you maintain a regular dosing schedule.

INFUSION THERAPY

Infusions are given in place of certain oral medications since traveling through the digestive system can make the drugs less effective. The lines are also used when medication needs to be administered at a specific pace.

+NOVANTRONE(mitoxantrone): Mitoxantrone appears to work by suppressing the body's immune system for the period of treatment, depleting the number of cells that are attacking the myelin around nerves.Most side effects of Novantrone are mild and include: Nausea and/or vomiting, diarrhea, constipation, heartburn, loss of appetite.



+OCREVUS(ocrelizumab): OCREVUS works on your immune system by targeting and removing specific B cells which play a role in MS. When targeted B cells are removed by OCREVUS, inflammation is reduced and the chance of other nerve cells begin affected is lessened.Ocrevus can have common and more serious side effects. Common side effects can include: Itchy skin with a rash, tiredness, coughing, throat pain, and fever.

+TYSABRI(natalizumab): it prevents harmful immune cells from crossing into the central nervous system (CNS), which may reduce damage and inflammation that relapsing MS causes in the brain. Common side effects of Tysabri can include sore throat, UTI, hives, dizziness, fever, and joint stiffness.

INJECTABLES

Injectable MS treatments come in two forms: self-injectables and intravenous infusions. Most injectables don’t have to be taken as often as oral medications, which are taken daily.

+ LEMATRADA(alemtuzumab): supresses myelin-damaging immune cells. Taken daily for 5 days; one year later, daily for three days. Some common side effects can include: nausea, vomiting, diarrhea.

+AVONEX(interferon beta-1a): This medication works as immune system modulator and has antiviral properties. Taken weekly via intramuscular injection. Common side effects include headache and flu-like symptoms.

+BETASERON(interferon beta-1b): It works as an immune system modulator and has antiviral properties. Taken every other day via subcutaneous injection. Common side effects include flu-like symptoms, low-white blood cell count.

+COPAXONE(glatiramer acetate): This works as a immune system modulator and blocks attack on myelin. It is taken daily or three times per week via subcutaneous injection. Common side effects can include flushing, shortness of breath, rash, and chest pain.

ORAL TREATMENTS



+AUBAGIO(teriflunomide): It works as an immune system modulator and inhibits nerve degeneration. Taken daily, however, common side effects can include headache, liver changes, nausea, and hair loss.

+GILENYA(fingolimod): Blocks T cells from leaving lymph nodes. Taken daily. Side effects include flu-like symptoms, and elevated liver enzymes.

+TECFIDERA(dimethyl fumarate): It includes anti-inflammatory properties, protects nerves and myelin from damage. Taken twice daily. Common side effects include GI changes, reduced WBC count, and elevated liver enzyme.
